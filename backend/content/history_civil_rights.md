# The Civil Rights Movement

## Fighting for Equality and Justice

The Civil Rights Movement was the struggle by African Americans for equal rights under the law in the 1950s and 1960s.

**Key Facts:**
- Peaked in the 1950s and 1960s
- Goal: end racial segregation and discrimination
- Used nonviolent protest methods
- Led by many brave people, including Dr. Martin Luther King Jr.
- Changed America forever with new laws protecting civil rights

**Background - Jim Crow Laws:**

**After Civil War:**
- 13th Amendment (1865) ended slavery
- 14th Amendment (1868) gave citizenship
- 15th Amendment (1870) gave voting rights to Black men

**But Then:**
- Southern states passed "Jim Crow" laws
- Enforced racial segregation (separation)
- "Separate but equal" (but not really equal!)
- African Americans couldn't vote in many areas
- Faced violence and intimidation

**What Segregation Looked Like:**
- Separate schools for Black and White students
- Separate water fountains, bathrooms, restaurants
- Blacks sat in back of buses
- Blacks couldn't stay in many hotels
- Many jobs closed to African Americans
- Couldn't vote due to literacy tests, poll taxes

**Major Events:**

**1. Brown v. Board of Education (1954)**
- Supreme Court case about school segregation
- Ruled "separate but equal" schools unconstitutional
- Major legal victory
- Many schools still resisted integration

**2. Montgomery Bus Boycott (1955-1956)**
- Rosa Parks refused to give up bus seat to White passenger
- Arrested for breaking segregation law
- African Americans boycotted buses for 381 days
- Walked, carpooled, rode bikes
- Supreme Court ruled bus segregation unconstitutional
- Made Martin Luther King Jr. famous

**3. Little Rock Nine (1957)**
- Nine Black students enrolled in Little Rock Central High School, Arkansas
- Governor tried to stop them with National Guard
- President Eisenhower sent Army to protect students
- Students faced harassment but persevered
- Showed courage in face of hatred

**4. Sit-Ins (1960)**
- Black college students sat at "Whites only" lunch counters
- Refused to leave when not served
- Spread across the South
- Peaceful protest despite insults and violence
- Eventually desegregated many lunch counters

**5. Freedom Rides (1961)**
- Black and White activists rode buses together through South
- Tested desegregation of interstate bus travel
- Faced violent mobs
- Some buses were burned
- Showed courage and commitment

**6. March on Washington (1963)**
- Over 250,000 people marched in Washington, D.C.
- Demanded jobs and freedom
- Dr. King gave "I Have a Dream" speech
- One of largest protests in US history
- Helped pass Civil Rights Act

**7. Birmingham Campaign (1963)**
- Protests in Birmingham, Alabama
- Police used fire hoses and dogs on peaceful protesters
- Photos shocked the nation and world
- Showed brutality of segregation

**8. Freedom Summer (1964)**
- Voter registration drive in Mississippi
- Three civil rights workers murdered
- Violence against activists
- Highlighted voting rights issues

**9. Selma to Montgomery Marches (1965)**
- Marched for voting rights
- "Bloody Sunday" - police attacked marchers on bridge
- Finally completed 54-mile march under protection
- Led to Voting Rights Act

**Important Laws Passed:**

**Civil Rights Act of 1964:**
- Outlawed discrimination based on race, color, religion, sex, or national origin
- Ended segregation in public places
- Banned employment discrimination
- President Lyndon B. Johnson signed it

**Voting Rights Act of 1965:**
- Banned literacy tests and poll taxes
- Federal oversight of voting in some states
- Dramatically increased Black voter registration
- Major victory for democracy

**Fair Housing Act of 1968:**
- Banned discrimination in housing
- Could no longer refuse to sell/rent based on race

**Key Leaders:**

**Dr. Martin Luther King Jr. (1929-1968)**
- Baptist minister and most famous civil rights leader
- Believed in nonviolent protest
- "I Have a Dream" speech
- Won Nobel Peace Prize (1964)
- Assassinated April 4, 1968 in Memphis
- We celebrate MLK Day (3rd Monday of January)

**Rosa Parks (1913-2005)**
- "Mother of Civil Rights Movement"
- Refused to give up bus seat
- Quiet courage sparked major movement
- Worked for civil rights her entire life

**Malcolm X (1925-1965)**
- Powerful speaker for Black rights
- Initially believed in separation, later changed views
- Advocated for Black pride and self-defense
- Assassinated 1965

**Thurgood Marshall (1908-1993)**
- Lawyer who argued Brown v. Board case
- First African American Supreme Court Justice (1967)
- Champion of civil rights through law

**John Lewis (1940-2020)**
- Freedom Rider, led Selma march
- Beaten many times for the cause
- Later served in Congress for 33 years
- Encouraged "good trouble"

**Ruby Bridges (born 1954)**
- First African American child to desegregate a school in the South (age 6)
- Faced angry mobs but went to school every day
- Symbol of courage

**The Children Who Marched:**
- Thousands of children and teenagers participated
- Faced arrest, violence, and harassment
- Showed incredible bravery
- Changed history

**Legacy:**

**What Changed:**
- Segregation laws ended
- Voting rights expanded
- More opportunities for African Americans
- Inspired other movements (women's rights, LGBTQ+ rights)

**Work Still to Do:**
- Inequality still exists
- Continued fight for justice
- Importance of standing up for what's right

**Methods of Protest:**
- Nonviolent civil disobedience
- Boycotts
- Sit-ins
- Marches
- Court challenges
- Voter registration
- All done peacefully despite violence against them
